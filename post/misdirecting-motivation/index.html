Overfocusing on Instrumentals

April 2022 · 3 minute read

For a while I used to deal with perpetual brain fog. While it didn’t completely undermine my ambitions, it was bad enough to warrant a solution, so I spent the next year or so experimenting with various behavioural and supplemental interventions. While I did notice improvements in my overall mood after more-or-less sticking with some of them, when it came to working and concentrating on the difficult things for which I was ultimately trying to optimise, I was still in a constant state of zombie-ness. The thing that annoyed me the most about this ordeal was that each time I sat down to do work well-rested, just-exercised and zooted on supplements, I was feeling as motivated as ever (which made sense), but that only a few minutes into the process fatigue took over. I finally seem to have found an explanation for what was the problem.

the confusion

Besides improving general brain health, a common theme in most of the nootropic interventions was to raise dopamine - the oft-misunderstood, but wonderful neurotransmitter that is responsible for motivation. My hope was that the boost in motivation that comes from dopamine-spike-inducing activities like exercising, listening to music, drinking coffee (or doing all 3 together) would carry over to the brainpower-demanding thing I was about to do. Little did I know that that is not how dopamine works at all…

enlightenment

In fact, what leads to a boost in subjective “motivation” is not the absolute level of dopamine in the brain, but rather the relative peak from the baseline. Naturally, the need for a relative raise in dopamine means that such bursts of motivation can only be intermittent. As such, inducing a dopamine spike by, for example, exercising before a study session is completely counterproductive, because what you’re in fact doing is carefully aligning the inevitable hard drop-off in motivation (which is proportional to the initial spike - meaning that my use of caffeine and tyrosine only made the situation worse) to the exact activity where its needed the most.

what to change

The cycle was vicious, because the dopamine hits I got out of doing the interventions were very real and I hence felt (confused, but) super-productive. But since those interventions alone were supposed to serve the single purpose of allowing me to direct more effort towards other stuff, from this perspective, drinking mythical nootropic concoctions and exercising was no better than indulging in other addictive behaviours like gambling and mindless scrolling of the web (not considering the genuine health benefits of embracing such a lifestyle, but that’s beside the point)
